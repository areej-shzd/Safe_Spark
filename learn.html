Understanding Epilepsy

Knowledge is power when it comes to seizure disorders

"IT'S NEVER TOO LATE TO START, IT'S ALWAYS TOO LATE TO WAIT"

Jeff Olson

What is epilepsy? +

Epilepsy, also known as 'seizure disorder' is a neurological condition characterized by sudden, random electrical discharges in the brain. This results in seizures, which are uncontrolled bursts of electrical activities that change sensations, behaviors, awareness and muscle movements. According to the World Health Organisation, an estimated 5 million people are diagnosed with epilepsy each year globally. This is a serious and lifelong condition that, if not treated correctly, can lead to serious injury or death.

What are the common causes for seizures? +

Seizure causes fall into two main categories depending on why they happen: Provoked seizures (nonepileptic) seizures involve a trigger, which could be a temporary condition or a certain circumstance. Unprovoked seizures (epileptic) are seizures that happen spontaneously (randomly). There are several possible causes for seizures. These can vary from the following: Medications, genetic factors, electrolyte abnormalities, sleep state, infections, brain inflammation, or injury.

What are common symptoms alongside seizures? +

Some common symptoms that occur in parallel to seizures include:
  • Loss of consciousness
  • Temporary confusion
  • Jerking body movements
  • Stiffening of the body
  • Breathing problems or stopping breathing
  • Loss of bowel or bladder control

Types of Epileptic Seizures +

Epileptic seizures are categorized into two main types: Focal seizures (starting in one area of the brain) and Generalized seizures (affecting both sides of the brain).
  • Focal aware seizures: Person remains conscious but may experience unusual feelings or movements
  • Focal impaired awareness seizures: Consciousness is affected, may include repetitive movements
  • Absence seizures: Brief lapses in consciousness, often in children
  • Tonic-clonic seizures: Violent muscle contractions and loss of consciousness
  • Myoclonic seizures: Sudden brief jerks of muscles

Diagnosis and Treatment Options +

Diagnosing epilepsy typically involves:
  • Detailed medical history and symptom description
  • Electroencephalogram (EEG) to measure brain activity
  • MRI or CT scans to identify structural brain abnormalities
  • Blood tests to rule out other conditions
Treatment options include:
  • Anti-seizure medications (about 70% of people respond well)
  • Ketogenic diet (high-fat, low-carb diet that helps control seizures)
  • Vagus nerve stimulation (implanted device that sends electrical impulses)
  • Responsive neurostimulation (implanted device that detects and stops seizures)
  • Surgery to remove the seizure focus in medication-resistant cases

First Aid for Seizures +

Do:
  • Stay calm and time the seizure
  • Clear the area of hard or sharp objects
  • Cushion the head with something soft
  • Turn the person gently onto their side (recovery position)
  • Stay with them until fully recovered
Don't:
  • Restrain the person or try to stop movements
  • Put anything in their mouth
  • Offer food/water until fully alert
  • Perform CPR unless they're not breathing after seizure ends
Call 911 if:
  • Seizure lasts more than 5 minutes
  • Person has difficulty breathing afterward
  • Person is injured during the seizure
  • It's their first seizure
  • Another seizure begins soon after the first

Living with Epilepsy +

Managing epilepsy involves more than just medical treatment. Important lifestyle considerations include:
  • Sleep: Maintain regular sleep patterns as fatigue can trigger seizures
  • Stress management: Practice relaxation techniques like meditation or yoga
  • Medication adherence: Take medications exactly as prescribed
  • Safety precautions: Avoid swimming alone, climbing ladders, or other high-risk activities
  • Seizure diary: Track triggers, frequency, and characteristics of seizures
  • Support systems: Connect with epilepsy support groups and communities
Many people with epilepsy live full, active lives with proper management. Open communication with employers, teachers, and loved ones is key to creating a safe environment.

Epilepsy Myths vs Facts +

Myth: You should put something in a seizing person's mouth to prevent swallowing their tongue.
Fact: This is dangerous and unnecessary. The tongue cannot be swallowed during a seizure.

Myth: Epilepsy is contagious.
Fact: Epilepsy cannot be "caught" from someone else.

Myth: All seizures involve convulsions.
Fact: Many seizures involve staring spells or subtle symptoms without convulsions.

Myth: People with epilepsy are mentally ill or intellectually disabled.
Fact: Epilepsy is a physical neurological condition unrelated to intelligence or mental health.

Myth: Epilepsy is always inherited.
Fact: While genetics can play a role, most cases have no family history.
